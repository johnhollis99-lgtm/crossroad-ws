The lighthouse keepers had a problem. From 1855 onward, the Old Point Loma Light stood at the very tip of San Diego's harbor — beautiful, photogenic, and almost completely useless. The lantern sat 462 feet above sea level, which sounds impressive until you realize that ships approaching from the Pacific spent most of their time looking up into the fog that hung against the cliffs, with the light hidden somewhere above it. After thirty-six years of frustrated mariners and increasingly diplomatic complaints, the Coast Guard gave up. They built a new lighthouse at the base of the bluff in 1891, where ships could actually see it, and abandoned the old one entirely. Today the Old Point Loma Light is a museum, restored to look the way it did in 1880 — the keeper's quarters, the spiral staircase, the original Fresnel lens. The view, if not the lighthouse, was always worth the climb.
