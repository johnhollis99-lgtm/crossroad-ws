The bell ringer steps out before sunrise, pulls the rope, and the sound carries across the valley the way it has since 1776. Mission San Juan Capistrano is the seventh of California's Spanish missions, and the only one where the original chapel — built by hand, of adobe and timber — is still standing. Father Junípero Serra said mass in this room. The Acjachemen people, whose ancestors had lived on this land for thousands of years before any Spanish ship ever appeared on the horizon, helped raise these walls; some are buried in the cemetery just outside. In 1812, a great earthquake brought down the larger stone church the mission was building next door, killing forty worshipers during the morning service. You can still walk through what's left of those ruins. And every spring, like clockwork, the cliff swallows return from Argentina to nest in the old stones — a migration of more than six thousand miles, ending here.
